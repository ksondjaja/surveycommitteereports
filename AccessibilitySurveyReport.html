Executive Summary

The Accessibility Interests Working Group (AIWG) of the Master of Information Student Council (MISC) requested the MISC Survey Committee to ask students about what AIWG can do to help improve accessibility awareness and accommodations for students at the Faculty of Information. The survey questions and form were designed in close collaboration with AIWG members.

Methods

The Accessibility Survey was launched on June 21 and was initially scheduled to end on July 4, during the Summer term. However, it was extended for another week to gather more responses from students, thus ending on July 11. The survey collected responses from 35 Master’s degree students at the Faculty of Information. It contained 2 required questions on participants’ consent to have their data collected and published, and 6 multiple-choice demographics questions regarding participants’ program and enrolment status (2 of which are required to filter qualified participants). All qualified participants were asked 5 optional questions (3 multiple-choice, 2 long-answer) about accessibility-related information and course content in iSchool, and 1 required multiple-choice question about whether they have personally been affected by accessibility needs at school. If the participant answered “no” or “I would prefer not to say”, the survey would end there. If the participant answered “yes”, they were asked to answer 5 more optional questions (1 multiple-choice, 4 long-answer) about their experience with accessibility at the iSchool. Because many questions are optional, the number of responses for each question varied.

The survey was promoted through MISC-y Business newsletter, iSchool’s Discord, and AIWG’s social media channels. Participants were incentivized with a chance to win a $10 digital gift card from a retailer of their choice if they entered a raffle at the end of the survey. There were 30 raffle entrants, including 1 entrant that was removed due to their failure to verify their UofT student status. A total of 2 verified raffle winners were picked.

Main Findings

Demographics

  • 97.1% (34) of 35 total participants were Master of Information (MI) students, while 1 was a Combined Degree Program (CDP) student.
  • UXD concentration students made up the biggest group, 37.8% (14), of all participants.
  • 48.6% (17) of participants began their iSchool program in 2020, while 34.3% (12) began in 2019 or earlier. 40.0% (14) of them chose to do co-op as a part of their program.
  • 70.6% (24) of participants were enrolled full-time, and only 11.8% (4 participants) were part-time students.

Accessibility Awareness & Course Content

  • 60.6% (20) of 33 respondents would contact Accessibility Services for any concern about accessibility they have at school, and 45.5% (15 respondents) would reach out to the Faculty of Information.
  • 14 of 18 respondents agreed that the topic of accessibility is not covered enough in iSchool courses, with most courses that mention it at all only covering the topic for only a week or two, instead of making the concept a core part of their study.
  • Participants commented that both instructors and students seem to have limited understanding of accessibility, and would like iSchool to cover the topic more holistically.
  • At least three-quarters of participants were interested in workshops and resources that would teach them accessibility-related practices that they can apply in their professional fields.

Personal Experience with Accessibility

  • 48.6% (17) of all participants reported that their educational experience has been affected by accessibility needs.
  • Respondents pointed out that, although most iSchool staff and instructors have been very helpful and accommodating, some staff and instructors’ limited understanding and biases about accessibility have negatively affected their educational experience.
  • Different course formats and methods of communication have either benefited or disadvantaged students with accessibility needs. For example, some respondents really struggle with doing group work, and some respondents feel accommodated by online course recordings.
  • Only 33.3% (5) of 15 respondents were registered with Accessibility Services.
  • Despite the majority of all respondents thinking that they should contact Accessibility Services for concerns, many respondents with personal experience of accessibility needs reported that the confusing information and complicated registration system have become a barrier to their success in registering with Accessibility Services and receiving accommodations they need.

Recommendations

  • Arrange events and resources to raise faculty members and students’ awareness of accessibility needs and its implications, and advocate to integrate accessibility as a core topic in the information field (instead of a “special topic”).
  • Forward student feedback about barriers to registration to Accessibility Services, such as how it requires too much paperwork, time, and official diagnoses that not all students in need could easily get or afford.
  • Encourage iSchool faculty members to better understand students’ accessibility needs and consider using Universal Design for Learning (UDL) guidelines, especially because students are facing concrete hurdles in getting registered to Accessibility Services in the first place.
  • Recommend iSchool to have a system to ensure a certain standard of accommodations across all courses, so that students do not have to solely depend on individual instructors’ understanding of the students’ needs.

2. Accessibility Awareness & Course Content

This section gathered feedback that AIWG aims to use to improve awareness and availability of knowledge on accessibility in the Faculty of Information.

Out of 33 responses, 60.6% (20 participants) would contact Accessibility Services for any concern about accessibility they have at school. A good 36.4% (12 participants) would reach out to AIWG. A couple of participants selected “Other” and commented that they would feel lost if they had such concerns and not reach out to anyone at school.

Who Participants would Contact about Accessibility Concerns at School

Figure 5. Who participants would contact about accessibility concerns at school

What is your opinion on the way that accessibility/disability topics are currently covered in the Master of Information program?

18 responses

There was a consensus among the 14 out of 18 participants that responded, that the topic of accessibility is not covered enough in their programs. Most courses that mention accessibility at all usually only have 1 or 2 weeks that cover it like a special topic, but accessibility is generally not treated as a core concept in the Faculty of Information courses. Many students and professors are still not used to discussing and thinking about it.

“I can tell some profs know more about the subject than others [...] while others are dependent on whatever literature they think relates.[...] There have been many times people have been silent when there's a class dedicated to it and I'm not sure whether that's from a lack of interest, knowledge, or from discomfort talking about it. It's quite disheartening.”

“Accessibility is very much still treated as lip service at the iSchool. [...] Some instructors are very invested and are super supportive, and then others don't care at all.”

A few students gave feedback on specific courses they have taken that are supposed to cover accessibility topics.

“I think there is room for accessibility/disability topics to be covered more. In INF2125 (Developing a Diversity Mindset in the Info Professions) we had a couple of guest speakers who talked about accessibility, but other than that the courses I have taken have not really covered accessibility/disability topics.”

“I think there is a lot of interest but excluding the great work that the AIWG does, this interest is very passive/shallow amongst students (I admit I am also guilty of this). Off the top of my head the only accessibility-related course that I know of is INF2165 Accessibility and Inclusive Design. I found this course enjoyable, but I've heard that the structure and experience of the course varies wildly depending on the sessional instructor leading it  and that it can come off as disorganized.”

The amount of coverage and focus of accessibility topics in courses also depend on concentration. 2 participants reported that HCDS concentration courses do not mention accessibility at all.

“Accessibility has almost always been a "Week 10" topic, lumped in with "Diversity". (I'm thinking specifically about my UXD courses; there has been almost no discussion of accessibility/disability in courses that don't have anything to do with UX).”

“Poorly in ISD and hardly at all so far in HCDS. Not as well or as thoroughly integrated as I imagined comparative to other concentrations. The disconnect is pretty clear.”

“Can't speak for the other concentrations, but LIS needs more coverage.”

Some participants pointed out the difference between discussing accessibility as an academic topic and as accommodations for individuals, such as getting extensions for assignments.

“I feel that instructors only understand accessibility in the context of "accessibility accommodations". I think this causes a lot of barriers for communication, especially when talking about remote learning, since instructors assume that accessibility services will just take care of things.”

Despite the vast majority of respondents agreeing that accessibility is not integrated in courses enough, 2 participants felt that there has been a lot of effort in the Faculty of Information to put accessibility in the curriculum and expose students to the topic.

Are there any changes you would like to see in what or how accessibility/disability content is included in the Master of Information Program?

10 responses

Most of the respondents to this question wrote that both students and professors still have a limited understanding of accessibility, especially in an academic context. For example, discussions might focus on only certain sides of accessibility. Participants commented that courses need to take a more holistic approach, such as covering the concept, history, approaches to solve accessibility problems specific to concentrations, and generally having more discussions about it.

“Maybe speaking beyond the mobility spectrum of disability? A lot of my museum classes tended not to focus on other disabilities in terms of accommodation. It always seems to be a second hand concern.”

“Having a week where accessibility is discussed, separate from the rest of the course, will not work for any course where you are continually talking about designing products. Additionally, leaving the inclusion of content entirely up to instructors with no consideration for or guidance from the overall concentration will result in repetitive, patchy, and ineffectively used accessibility/disability content.”

Some students criticized how accessibility is being treated like a novelty, trendy, or niche topic, while it should be integrated as a core concept in the information field.

“We need to stop treating 'Accessibility' like a special interest [...] like, a new, growing part of UX, and I've noticed that a lot of fellow students hear that and think ‘this is something I can use to get an edge on other job seekers’. [...] It's something that should be thought about for every lecture, and beyond content, too. How is lecture material made accessible, how are professors accessible to their students?”

“I would like to see accessibility/disability included in all mandatory courses. The sooner that our students learn about accessibility the better. It shouldn't be some wild awakening for them that people with disabilities exist and are affected by their discipline.”

One commenter expressed that they do not feel that they are the best person to answer this question, confirming the idea that iSchool students are not confident about their knowledge of accessibility.

Events about Accessibility that Participants are Interested in

The majority of 30 respondents to the question on accessibility events were interested in having informative events about accessibility, especially workshops on inclusive practices (86.7% or 26 respondents). A couple of responses expressed interest in events targeted specifically for students with disabilities and/or accessibility needs.

Similarly, when asked about what resources they are interested in, the vast majority of respondents were interested in learning practical skills to address accessibility problems in their fields. 90.3% (28) of 31 respondents were interested in guidelines for inclusive professional practices. However, 41.9% (13 participants) were also interested in more general information about disability, accessibility and accommodations at UofT.

3. Personal Experience with Accessibility

This section gathered feedback that AIWG intends to use for improving school experience for iSchool students with accessibility needs.

48.6% (17) of all 35 survey participants answered that their educational experience has been affected by their physical or mental health, disability, or any accessibility needs. We asked these 17 participants to answer additional optional questions about their experience in iSchool.

Whether Participants’ Educational Experience Has Been Affected by Physical/Mental Health, Disability, or Any Accessibility Needs

Figure 8. Whether participants’ educational experience has been affected by accessibility needs

What barriers related to physical/mental health, accessibility, or disability have you faced at the iSchool?

12 responses

Half of the respondents commented on how the school system or instructors have indirectly penalized them, either academically or financially, for having disabilities and/or health conditions. Some respondents added how the school or some instructors lack real understanding of students’ conditions, and their biases make students feel excluded or discouraged.

“I’ve had a prof  tell me that I should continue working on a project after my grade had been submitted for a class despite telling the prof that my mental health was declining significantly. [...] I have significant mental health concerns. And my prof knew that.”

“My disability has affected my ability to work at times, resulting in resume weak spots. [...] I had a couple of experiences where the career services counsellors were a bit insensitive - not understanding that there really were times in the past when I did nothing, and that it wasn't a lack of competence or will [...] Maintaining that type of unfaltering self-confidence is very different when you have a condition that leads people to write you off as useless whenever you get sick.”

“Some instructors emphasize in the syllabus or mention during the first class that studies have reported that students "do better" without technology, with the insinuation that technology during lecture is distracting. [...There is an] unspoken implication that I would be judged for my technology use if I don't have a valid (ie. registration with accessibility services) reason. [...Instructors should keep] in mind individuals who may be using technology to cope with undiagnosed and untreated conditions.”

“Some course instructors do not include accessibility statements in their syllabus which makes me think they do not want disabled students in their class. [...] The large amount of group work assigned can make working out accommodations difficult.”

Almost half of the respondents also mentioned how certain methods of communication or course formats could be helpful or, oppositely, a barrier for their specific conditions. One student added that they have not been provided the accommodation they requested despite being registered to Accessibility Services.

“Group work has been particularly difficult for me. I'm embarrassed to tell my group mates that I need accommodations or extra support, but then I seem like just a bad partner. Additionally, I've asked for note takers in every class I've taken at the iSchool and never received one.”

“Once I had a bad connection and the lecture was not recorded and also did not have closed caption on BB Collaboration. As a result I lost the lecture’s important content.”

“Email has benefited me in that it let's me look over my words and let's me be more intentional with my phrasing. Communication can be a barrier for me, and while I'd like to think I'm improving, there are times I just can't process part of what I'm being told.”

Other comments mentioned how having previous bad experiences in attempting to gain accommodations, strict registration deadlines for Accessibility Services, long waiting times for school mental health services, and lack of information of who qualifies for such services are all making their educational experience more difficult. These are very similar barriers that many participants brought up in the Mental Health Survey (conducted May 31 - June 17, 2021).

“I am a caregiver for a parent who has a serious illness. My grief has profoundly affected my mental health while getting my Masters remotely. I am unsure if I qualify as someone who can take advantage of accessibility services or not.”

How has the iSchool helped with barriers related to your physical/mental health, accessibility needs, or any form of disability?

9 responses

Despite student complaints of how parts of the school system or some instructors lack consideration of students’ disabilities or conditions, most of the respondents to this question also answered that many staff and instructors have been very accommodating and try their best to understand students’ accessibility needs. In addition, a few students mentioned again that certain course formats have been helpful for their accessibility needs.

“My mental health has taken a dive due to my circumstances, making completing assignments a major task (no matter their size). All of my professors have been extremely accommodating (i.e., approving my requested extensions) without me needing to divulge too much information on my end, which has made my experience as a remote student more enjoyable/manageable.”

“Instructors have been really kind when it comes to accommodating requests for extensions. Instructors that presented materials creatively (e.g. flipped classroom) and in ways to increase engagement, both in-person and online, through activities and discussions have been very appreciated!”

“Having recordings of classes was very helpful. My condition can affect attendance [...] As someone who believes in the importance of hearing it yourself whenever possible and wants to take their own notes, recordings were the best thing to be offered.”

What could the iSchool do to reduce barriers related to your physical/mental health, accessibility needs, or any form of disability?

11 responses

Some participants suggested for the school to make it easier for students to get accommodations or register with Accessibility Services, once again revealing how students with disabilities and/or accessibility needs may already face many barriers in getting official diagnosis or navigating through the healthcare system and registration process for Accessibility Services.

“Make it feel less like you need to prove a case to access accessibility services.”

“Grant more compassionate assignment extensions without proof of medical notes.”

Respondents also called to raise awareness of both faculty members and students about accessibility needs, as not all faculty members seem to have an in-depth understanding of the issue, and students may not realize when they need assistance themselves.

“Do a better job at training faculty members about mental health related disability.”

“Encourage students to support each other, get professors to not be so precious about their academic freedom and intellectual property and just give disabled students what they need. There should be an ethos at the school throughout the faculty, admin, and students that recognizes student difference and works to cultivate it rather than punish students for facing barriers.”

“I wonder if students need to see real-life cases that require accessibility needs. I feel like there are students who are suffering, but they don't believe they qualify for accessibility services because it isn't as 'serious' as other, more well-known physical/mental illnesses.”

Some participants suggested for iSchool to edit some of its policies, such as having instructors release course syllabi earlier, not financially penalizing students that need to take lighter course loads due to their conditions, and having additional accessibility resources just for iSchool students.

“I am careful not to overload myself, but this pricing makes it difficult. For my health, ideally I would take 1.5 FCE each in the fall and winter terms, and 0 FCE in the summer so I could dedicate myself to an internship. If I did my degree this way, I would end up paying somewhere between  $4,200 and $8,200 more than the minimum degree fee, so I am not doing this. [...] The iSchool could put in place policies about syllabus release. [...]I want to read the syllabus and see if the class will be a good fit for my accommodation requirements.”

“Have more faculty based resources as campus ones are more often than not insufficient.”

Whether Participants are Registered with Accessibility Services

Figure 9. Whether participants are registered with Accessibility Services

The final part of the survey asked a couple of questions specifically about UofT’s Accessibility Services. Out of 15 respondents, only 5 were registered with Accessibility Services, while 4 were interested in or attempted to register but felt discouraged or did not successfully register. In their long-answer comments, even some students that selected “No, I’m not interested in registering” expressed that it is because they felt pessimistic about what Accessibility Services could really do to help them.

Have you faced barriers in registering for or receiving accommodations and accessibility services at the University of Toronto?

7 responses

As mentioned in a previous section, many students found the registration process and information regarding UofT’s Accessibility Services to be overly complicated, long, and confusing. This reputation discourages some students from completing their registration, and some even prefer to approach individual instructors to provide accommodations, even though they may not be equally helpful.

“I have not [registered] personally, because I have heard from classmates that it's almost impossible to receive accommodations from accessibility services if you're not already 'accepted' by them [...] I'd rather just work with whatever my professors are willing to give me and deal with the grades I get than put the huge effort into learning about what I need, compiling what I need, and then presenting what I need to accessibility services knowing that they could very well decide that they're not willing to help me.”

“I was confused by the medical form. What in the world is a "primary disability"? [...] There does not seem to be a general, consistent understanding of what different listed accommodations require from instructors. [...] It was unclear if I was supposed to fill out the ATS form for every quiz, and the form has a 2-week deadline that doesn't always work with professors' desire to move or schedule quizzes throughout the term. Some of my professors hated ATS's "help" so much that they asked that I just go through the prof/TA, who could easily add time to an online quiz, allowing us to ignore ATS entirely.”

Respondents specified that required paperwork for registration is often tied to getting a certain diagnosis. For some conditions, this could require receiving external healthcare and paying additional medical expenses, thus delaying the registration process.

“It took me a long time to register, and cost a lot of money. I didn't have health care when I first started school, so I had to pay out of pocket. I didn't get accommodations until my second semester despite spending around $500 out of pocket to get paperwork.”

“It seemed like it would take a long time, and the requirements for ADHD involved information I did not yet have.”

“[Accessibility Services makes] me feel like my circumstances (anticipatory grief) aren't deserving of accommodation, despite it being a major influence in my life. When I look to see what UofT can provide, all I see is a grief sharing circle, which isn't much use to me [...] A lot of the provided resources concerning grief direct me to external links outside of the university, which makes looking for help all the more frustrating.”
