Executive Summary

The Master of Information Student Council (MISC) Survey Committee was asked by the MISC Mental Health Committee to gather data and feedback on how to better support students’ mental health.

Methods

The Survey Committee collected 61 valid anonymous responses from the Mental Health Survey, which was conducted during the Summer term, from May 31 to June 17. The survey targeted Master program students at the Faculty of Information. It contained a total of 27 questions, including 6 demographic questions, 11 multiple-choice questions, and 10 long-answer questions. All questions were optional, thus the number of responses to each question varied. 3 of the raw 64 submissions were removed because one of them answered no questions, another seemed to be spam based on the answers, and the other was a duplicate entry.

Participants were incentivized with a chance to win a $15 digital gift card from a retailer of their choice if they entered a raffle at the end of the survey. The survey was initially promoted on all of MISC’s social media platforms, but because the first version was spammed by hundreds of non-University of Toronto students who just wanted to enter the raffle, the survey was reset and promoted only on MISC’s newsletter and private online communities. Students who entered the first spammed raffle with University of Toronto e-mail addresses were automatically reentered into the new raffle, and were encouraged to resubmit their survey entry as well as another raffle entry. 4 raffle winners were picked out of 65 raffle entries, which include the said duplicate entries to incentivize resubmission.

Main Findings

Demographics

  • 96.7% (59) of the total 61 participants were Master of Information students, while the remaining 2 participants were Combined Degree Program students.
  • LIS concentration students made up the biggest group with a total of 25 participants, followed by UXD with 16 participants, and ARM with 13 participants.
  • As with most other MISC surveys, participants consisted of small groups of part-time students (18.3% or 11 people) and international students (9.8% or 6 people). Only 3.3% or 2 participants were domestic students from outside Ontario.
  • 17 of 38 respondents claimed that their mental health is in a bad condition, 14 claimed it is “ok” or “pretty good”, and 7 replied that it varies. No participant answered more positively than “pretty good”.

Mental Health Resources

  • 37.7% (23) of 61 participants have used UofT mental health resources, and about three-quarters of them have used UofT Health & Wellness counselling, therapy or psychiatric care.
  • Only 29.2% (7) of 24 respondents have used Accessibility Services, and student comments complained that UofT’s websites and registration systems for their mental health resources are difficult to navigate, especially for students that are already suffering from symptoms of mental health issues. Some students find it so difficult that they fail to register.
  • A portion of students that have received counselling through UofT complained that the treatment is too short-term for their mental health conditions, and that it is difficult to find affordable external mental health treatment due to their limited health insurance coverage and lack of information.

Checking In on Your Mental Health

  • Based on 59 responses, the most common factors that negatively affect participants’ mental health were balancing various responsibilities (69.5% or 41 respondents), the current social/political situation (62.7% or 37 respondents), and self-esteem (61.0% or 36 respondents).
  • Oppositely, the most common positive factors for mental health among 57 respondents were relationships (77.2% or 44 respondents) and family (63.2% or 36 respondents).
  • Participants listed physical activities, taking a walk outside their homes, connecting with other people, making time for hobbies & self-care, and prioritizing their wellbeing over work or school as habits that help them improve their mental health.

MISC's Mental Health Initiatives

  • 47.7% (27) of 57 respondents were interested in joining a student mental health support group.
  • Almost all students interested in the support group were open to being in a group with fellow iSchool students. 13 students were open to be in a support group with students from other faculties. 22 of these students were open to communicating with the group digitally.
  • More than 80% of respondents would like to hear about MISC mental health events by email or newsletter. Out of 49 responses, students expressed most interest in events on how to access mental health resources (65.3% or 32 respondents), self-care events or strategies (63.3% or 31 respondents), and how to manage mental health at school or work (57.1% or 28 respondents).

Recommendations

  • Create events, Q&A’s, or informational material to explain step-by-step what students need and how to register with Accessibility Services and UofT Health & Wellness services.
  • Share information on how to find affordable external mental health care resources for students that still need therapy or treatments beyond those available at UofT.
  • Forward student complaints about limited mental health care insurance coverage to UTGSU.
  • Advocate to make on-campus mental health care more immediately available and more helpful to students that need long-term care.
  • Invite mental health professionals to speak at events that teach students how to do self-care and maintain their mental health as students and professionals.

Before participants proceeded to more detailed questions on the next page, they were also asked two general optional long-answer questions about their definition of mental health and general mental well-being.

What does mental health mean to you?

23 responses

Of the 23 participants that responded to this question, 11 people stated that their mental health is about how well their mind is functioning, and 4 people wrote that it affects how well they function overall in their lives.

“Mind being able to function normally without being overwhelmed by depression.”

8 respondents highlighted how mental health is linked to how well they are able to recover from stress and setbacks, and 8 mentioned that it affects their outlook on life.

“Positive outlook, ability to rebound from lows.”

4 participants highlighted the importance of having a good work-school-life balance to maintain their mental wellbeing. 2 people added that they need time to deal with stress and recover.
“It means maintaining a healthy work/school/life balance. Taking necessary breaks and making time for self-care is key to mental health.”
In addition, 4 participants wrote that having good mental health means that they do not have to “worry”, feel “guilt”, or be “weighed down” in their daily lives or when they decide to take a break. This could signify that a number of participants might already be affected by anxiety to some extent.

“Mental health means being able to comfortably relax without guilt.”

How is your mental health?

38 responses

Among the 38 participants that responded, 17 wrote that their mental health is in a bad condition, 14 claimed it is “ok” or “pretty good”, and 7 replied that it varies or “depends” on the situation. Interestingly, no participants answered “great” or an equally very positive reply. A possible reason is that students that decided to participate in this survey tend to be those that already have some mental health concerns, thus more eager to give feedback on resources that they need to use.

In further details, 5 participants answered that their mental health has been affected by the pandemic. A few of these participants pointed out how the pandemic has worsened their pre-existing mental health conditions and circumstances.

“Not being in person has taken a serious toll on my mental health as digital spaces are really anxiety provoking for me. As someone with social cue issues, digital communication prevents me from feeling connected and confident when engaging in learning. My anxiety and depression levels have been higher and with people passing away from covid as well as other ailments, it has been hard to process everything this semester.”

“Not great. Because of the pandemic and my financial status I'm living at home, which is a location that strongly triggers my PTSD.”

5 participants mentioned how school and work directly affects their mental health. For some students, this included concerns about job prospects after graduation.

“During school, horrible and hanging by a thread. Now, I feel good enough to feel capable to handle most things although the state that school puts me in definitely requires some recovery time.”

“It's really hard to feel optimistic about anything right now to be honest, academically or professionally. I keep thinking about how difficult it's going to be to find a job post graduation. And it's just so hard to plan for anything right now when everything is still so up in the air.”

In addition, a participant responded that their mental health is positively affected by the warming season/weather, and another participant has benefited from having social connection.

“Still a little [depressed], but better since I've found someone to share my life and feelings with.”

2. Mental Health Resources

This section asked whether participants have used University of Toronto or external mental health resources, and what barriers they face, if any, in accessing these resources.

Out of 61 participants, only 37.7% (23 people) answered that they have used a University of Toronto mental health resource. Meanwhile, out of 60 responses, 51.7% (31 people) have used external mental health resources. For each question, 2 and 1 participant respectively chose not to disclose. There could be participants that have used only UofT resources, only external resources, or both of them.

UofT Mental Health Resources that Participants Have Used

Figure 6. UofT mental health resources used by participants

75% (18) out of 24 respondents answered that they have used UofT Health & Wellness counseling, therapy, or psychiatric care. Much fewer people have used any other school-affiliated mental health services. Only 29.2% (7 people) have used Accessibility services, and only 25.0% (6 people) have used single-session counseling services, such as MySSP. Only 1 participant has used MISC Mental Health Committee resources.

Reasons for Not Using Mental Health Resources (UofT or External)

Figure 7. Reasons for not using mental health resources

In response to why they have not used certain mental health resources, whether UofT or external, students’ most common reason, with 41.2% (14) out of 34 responses, was that they are unaware of resources’ availability or how to use them. This reason was closely followed by feeling uncomfortable using the resources (38.2% or 13 respondents), not feeling like they need the resources to manage (38.2% or 13 respondents), and not having time to use the resources (35.3% or 12 respondents). Another relatively common issue was that the cost of mental health resources are too high (23.5% or 8 respondents).

More details about the barriers that participants faced in trying to use mental resources were revealed through their answers to the following three long-answer questions:

If you would like to elaborate on your reasons, please feel free to share.

12 responses

If you HAVE used a mental health resource in UofT, how do you feel about your experience?

17 responses

What would help you gain access to mental health resources more easily or improve your overall experience?

25 responses

Difficulties in accessing UofT Health & Wellness and Accessibility Services

One of the top issues that students commented on was the difficulty they face in navigating and successfully registering for UofT Health & Wellness counseling and Accessibility Services. Participants claimed that the website and registration system are confusing, wait time for counseling is too long, registration deadlines are too strict, and there are too many steps and paperwork they have to go through to finally access services. These factors further discourage students that are already dealing with symptoms of mental health problems. Several students claimed that they failed to access these resources altogether due to the barriers.

“I was unable to figure out how to actually book an online session or to sign up.”

“Accessibility Services and the Care system are great...once you get connected to them. But getting access to it is such a hassle, I couldn't even figure out how to properly do it from the website, I had to have my student advisor help me connect. I just feel like the language and the organization of the site isn't clear, and all the forms and verifications makes you feel intimidated and shies people away from accessing help, particularly if you have depression or anxiety where you're already in a paralyzed, self-questioning state as it is.”

“[There should be] quicker response time, like if I’m feeling like I need help now, or going through a crisis (no imminent danger just not a great time) that I have to spend the time finding someone, then calling or emailing and waiting for a response, then getting on a waitlist, then getting an appointment, then finally talking to someone after like a month.”

“My doctor wanted me to get accessibility accommodations in March when my father was sick and my depression was very severe, but it was after the February deadline and I was unable to receive any accommodations.”

The paperwork requirements for registering also could cause privacy issues for some students, such as those that still depend on their families for access to healthcare.

“I was trying to access counselling at Health and Wellness a few years ago, but when I was at the counter with all my paperwork, the receptionist told me I also needed a referral from my GP and that the process could take a few months - I didn't end up going through with it because it's difficult for me to reach my GP without my family being aware (and I'd rather keep my reasons for seeking counselling to myself).”

Most of the students that commented about difficulties navigating through the system were domestic Ontario students living in EST time zone, which shows how access problems are not limited to students living far away from the campus. One participant felt that information on resources are too limited to those available in Toronto, and another participant mentioned how some resources are even more inaccessible for fellow students located outside Canada.

“[There should be] more services that are more affordable, and knowledge of resources throughout the GTA, not just in downtown Toronto.”

“I've also had friends be unable to access the services because they're not in the country. [...] As far as I'm aware they're still paying the school to support these services, so why is it that they don't have access to, literally anything?”

Of the 25 participants that provided suggestions on what would help them gain access to mental health resources, 10 commented that they want to see improvements to the website or, at least, additional help and clearer explanation for how to access and navigate through UofT’s mental health care system and Accessibility Services.

Free UofT counseling is too short-term

Among the students that were able to get counseling at UofT, some still felt that the treatment is too short-term. For those dealing with more serious mental health conditions, this is insufficient.

“iSchool counsellor is available as an ‘issue’-based service, which is fine when trying to deal with one issue or challenge, but not adequate for on-going support or when multiple issues are leading to feeling overwhelmed.”

“The service is quite brusque. This is not necessarily bad, but it is not particularly comforting. The sessions were short term and techniques were quite rote. I would look elsewhere for additional assistance, but it was a good starting place.”

Long-term therapy or treatment is too costly

The fact that the free counseling available at UofT is insufficient forces some students to seek therapy or other mental health treatments outside school. However, students complained that this is too costly, and their insurance coverage is too little. Some commented that they had to stop their treatment due to this financial barrier.

“I worked with a psychotherapist external to UofT until my benefits ran out - after that point, I could not afford to continue.”

“[Health insurance] coverage would get me around 3 sessions with a counsellor, which is not enough to develop any meaningful relationship let alone make any meaningful progress.”

Quality of mental health resources

Students gave mixed feedback on the quality of counseling, with most people saying that it is “ok” or “good”, but only for addressing short-term problems. However, a few students commented about having bad experiences.

“It was very poor! The first therapist I saw (iSchool embedded therapist) refused to help me unless I tried medication. I then saw someone else who saw me twice, prescribed me medication, then retired without warning and with no refills on a prescription.”

Because many students already reported feeling uncomfortable with using mental health resources, having a bad or disappointing experience with mental health services could further discourage students from seeking help again.

“Scared that I won’t get the proper help I need.”

“I had issues when I was younger and have learned coping mechanisms that are better than what mental health resources are able to provide.”

Need to increase awareness of mental health and normalize seeking help

Several participants suggested improving mental health awareness, which would also help remove the stigma from seeking help & resources and make them more accessible. One participant highlighted that even the current efforts for raising awareness is insufficient in making the community truly understand mental health struggles.

“Sometimes I feel issues are talked at rather than fully [understood] (can happen both in classes and with speakers). The worst part is that most of the time I feel like it's from ignorance and not purposeful attacks and so I tell myself not to fight back, but it's all so tiresome. This varies from physical/mental conditions to just making judgmental remarks about someone's background (employment, demographic, etc.) This has even happened with mental health resources at other schools causing me to distrust their support.”

3. Checking In on Your Mental Health

This section asked about the state of participants’ mental health, as well as factors that contribute to it.

69.5% (41) out of 59 respondents claimed that balancing various responsibilities is negatively affecting their mental health. In connection to this, more than 44% of participants also selected employment, school work, family, and/or finances as negative factors. Other negative external factors specific to circumstances in the past year or so, such as current social/political situation (62.7% or 37 respondents) and internet use (54.2% or 32 respondents) also ranked high.

However, many participants also selected very personal factors, such as self-esteem (61.0% or 36 respondents), mental health condition (47.5% or 28 respondents) and physical health condition (28.8% or 17 respondents). Overall, many factors that negatively affect participants’ mental health are somewhat connected to the pandemic, school, work & family responsibilities, and threats to their emotional, physical & social security.

Factors that NEGATIVELY Affect Participants’ Mental Health

Figure 8. Negative factors to mental health

If you would like to elaborate on the specific challenges you face, please feel free to share.

9 responses

Most participants listed multiple negative factors to their mental health, and participants’ additional comments mentioned news and social issues as adding onto their stress. These included pandemic news & response, discrimination of trans people, hate crimes towards Asian people, and public disagreements over social issues. Several other participants listed feeling overwhelmed by academic & professional competition, juggling numerous responsibilities, and dealing with other people’s ignorance.

“I'm part of the caregiving team for an ill parent, working multiple jobs, and I've had several serious health issues throughout my degree (prior to covid). I don't think this is particularly atypical but there is a siloed nature to the available resources, or even just the sense that mentioning your ill parent is uncouth, or mentioning your other job is rude, etc. Or even just using ‘caregiver’ to really mean just ‘parent’, not people who are other kinds of caregivers.”

Factors that Contribute POSITIVELY to Participants’ Mental Health

Figure 9. Positive factors to mental health

When asked what things positively contribute to their mental health, the top factors that participants selected are relationships (77.2% or 44 respondents) and family (63.2% or 36 respondents). Many participants also selected factors such as employment, food security, community, safety, and housing. Although 32 participants selected internet use as a negative factor to their mental health in a previous question, 14 participants claimed to be positively affected by it. The results showed that having meaningful connections and a sense of security contribute positively to participants’ mental health.

What resources, habits, or activities, if any, have you found useful for improving your mental health?

34 responses

Out of 34 responses, 18 listed doing some kind of physical activity or exercise as a helpful way to improve their mental health. This included taking a walk, yoga, running, working out, and just being outside the house for a change of scenery. A couple of people also mentioned the importance of getting enough sleep.

“Exercise, consistent sleep schedule, and lowering your expectations.”

Another helpful activity listed by 10 participants was connecting with other people, whether they are family, partners, friends, or new acquaintances.

“Talking to my partner, and my best friend about everything. Couldn't do it without them.”

10 more participants wrote that hobbies or doing fun activities, such as reading, journaling, watching movies, and taking care of plants, have helped improve their mental health. In connection to this, 6 participants highlighted that they need to make a conscious effort to make time to do such self-care activities, and 7 commented on the importance of prioritizing their own wellbeing over school or work.

“Reading has been my lifeline this past year, but also running outside. It's nice to have something to get me out of the house, especially because it's easy to just stay inside and stew in all the negative emotion.”

“Remembering to take breaks, practicing self-care, dressing up even when at home, taking walks.”

“Remembering that work and school are just means to live, not your whole life.”

Other helpful practices that participants wrote about included getting professional help for their mental & physical health, practicing mindfulness, being more aware of their emotions, eating a healthy diet, limiting screen time, and having time just for themselves.

4. MISC’s Mental Health Initiatives

This section asked for participants’ feedback and suggestions for MISC Mental Health Committee’s events and initiatives, such as arranging a mental health support group for students.

In this section, participants were asked whether they would be interested in having a mental health support group at school. Out of 57 participants that answered, 47.4% (27) were interested. Furthermore, 32 participants answered the question of what kind of students they would like to be in a support group with. 50.0% (16) of respondents would like to be in a support group with other iSchool students, while 37.5% (12) wanted to be in a group with students from both iSchool and other faculties.

Who Participants Want to be in a Mental Health Support Group with

Figure 10. Who participants want to be in a mental health support group with

Participants’ preference of communication method with the support group was somewhat mixed, with 39.3% (11) of 28 respondents being open to either digital or in-person communication, and an equal number of respondents preferring only digital communication.

How Participants Want to Communicate with the Mental Health Support Group

Figure 11. How participants want to communicate with mental health support group

If you are interested in a support group, what topics would you like to talk about with your peers?

11 responses

When asked in more detail about what they would like to talk about in a support group, a few people expressed that they are unsure or have no experience being in a similar group. A couple of people expressed some discomfort of sharing private concerns, especially among peers that they might continue to have professional relationships with.

However, participants that were more enthusiastic expressed that they would like to talk with peers, gain support, and share resources & strategies on managing school work, responsibilities, and dealing with mental health.

How Participants Want to Hear about MISC Mental Health Events

Figure 12. How participants want to hear about MISC Mental Health Events

In addition to the support group idea, the survey asked for students’ preferences on MISC mental health events. The vast majority of 48 respondents (81.2% or 39 people) preferred email communication, followed by MISC’s newsletter (47.9% or 23 people), which is also distributed by email.

Out of 49 responses, the events that participants seemed to be most interested in were about how to access mental health resources (65.3% or 32 votes), self-care events or strategies (63.3% or 31 votes), and how to manage mental health at school or work (57.1% or 28 votes).

Types of MISC Mental Health Events Participants are Interested In

Figure 13. MISC Mental Health Events that participants are interested in

What mental health initiatives do you think MISC should prioritize?

15 responses

Several people commented that MISC should prioritize helping students afford therapy or professional mental health care, whether it is through explaining how to use student healthcare benefits, helping students find affordable mental health care in their local areas, connecting students to some other financial assistance, or just advocating for more on-campus counseling and therapy to be available to students.

“Ask SGS to increase benefits coverage.”

“Having real professionals to help students. continued long-term therapy sessions.”

Some participants restated the need to make Accessibility Services and other UofT mental health resources more available to students. Other participants wanted MISC to prioritize events that teach strategies on how to do self-care and manage stress. Several others wrote that raising awareness of mental health issues should be prioritized.

Do you have any comments or suggestions on how else MISC could help students with their mental health?

4 responses

As for additional suggestions, respondents emphasized how students need to be better informed and connected to UofT or external mental health resources that they could use. A respondent also appreciated that MISC is keeping the communication channel about mental health open.

“Foster connections with internal and external support resources.”

“I think keeping communications open, as this survey is doing, is a valuable fundamental that MISC can engage in. Individuals  have to take responsibility for their health by thinking about it and practicing communicating about it. Surveys like this create and invite that practice.”

Introduction

This survey is intended for all current Master's students at the University of Toronto, Faculty of Information. The aim of this survey is to gauge students’ mental health needs and how MISC Mental Health Committee could serve and represent student interests. This survey might take about 20 minutes to complete. Survey responses are anonymous and do not ask for any identifying information. Your participation in this survey is much appreciated!

The Mental Health survey will include some sensitive questions about personal mental health that may be triggering or upsetting to some participants. Our purpose for asking such questions is to locate resources and provide help for students in various circumstances. Your participation in this survey is completely optional, and you may skip any question you do not want to answer. You may leave the survey anytime, and if you decide to do so, we will not save your answers. If you do submit the survey, your answers will be completely anonymous and kept confidential. Your answers will be used only to guide MISC Mental Health Committee in allocating time and resources to address student needs, and we will not share or reuse your answers for other purposes.

You may enter a draw for a $15 digital gift card at the end of the survey.

Demographics

  1. What is your program at iSchool?
  2. What is your year?
  3. What is your concentration?
  4. What is your enrollment status?

  5. If you would like to elaborate on your reasons, please feel free to share.
  6. If you HAVE used a mental health resource in UofT, how do you feel about your experience?
  7. What would help you gain access to mental health resources more easily or improve your overall experience.
